How the Literature Evolved

Bowen (1953)
"Father of CSR". Coined the concept of social responsibilities of businessmen.
Carroll (1991)
Developed the 4-part Pyramid (Economic, Legal, Ethical, Philanthropic).
Porter & Kramer (2011)
Introduced "Creating Shared Value" (CSV) - aligning business & social goals.
Stakeholder Theory
Stakeholder Theory (Freeman, 1984)
Defines tribal communities as primary stakeholders whose perceptions of CSR are the dependent variable.
Two-Way Symmetrical Model
Two-Way Symmetrical Model (Grunig & Hunt, 1984)
Tests whether dialogue-based communication (Involvement Strategy) achieves higher perceived betterment of society than one-way messaging.
Agenda-Setting Theory
Agenda-Setting Theory (McCombs & Shaw, 1972)
Analyzes whether corporate communication priorities match what tribal communities actually need.
Diffusion of Innovations
Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers, 1962)
Examines how CSR awareness spreads through tribal communities via opinion leaders like Gram Pradhan and Sarpanch.

National Sectoral Focus Review

Literature Consensus

Historically, Indian CSR is heavily skewed towards Education and Healthcare, primarily because these offer highly visible, easily quantifiable outputs (e.g., number of buildings, medical camps).

Environment and Livelihood initiatives, while critical in tribal belts, receive less funding and suffer from complex, long-term communication challenges.

CSR in Indian Legislative Context

Companies Act, 2013

Section 135 made India the first country to legally mandate CSR expenditure (2% rule).

CSR Rules, 2014

Defined Schedule VII activities, board composition, and the "Comply or Explain" mechanism.

2021 Amendments

Mandated Independent Impact Assessments for projects > ₹1 Crore. Shifted focus from spending to impact.

Tribal Context of CSR in India

Challenges

  • Historical trust deficit due to land displacement
  • Linguistic barriers (Gondi, Santhali, etc.)
  • Top-down corporate planning ignoring local needs
  • Digital divide limiting electronic communication

Opportunities

  • Leveraging traditional community structures (Gram Sabha)
  • Integration with PESA Act and Forest Rights Act
  • Local knowledge systems in environmental CSR
  • High impact potential on Human Development Index

CSR in Chhattisgarh & Jharkhand — Literature

Chhattisgarh

Key Findings (Sinha 2018, IJSDR 2023)

  • High CSR fund generation due to mining (SECL, NMDC, BALCO)
  • Funds often diverted to urban infrastructure rather than affected rural areas
  • Significant gap between "reported compliance" and "beneficiary perception"

Core Issue Companies focus heavily on government relations (PR) rather than communicating directly with tribal beneficiaries

Jharkhand

Key Findings (Sharma 2019, Swaniti 2022)

  • Tata Steel Foundation presents a benchmark model, but heavily concentrated in East Singhbhum
  • Severe regional disparity in fund allocation across districts
  • Heavy reliance on institutional and print media which fails in low-literacy remote areas

Core Issue A highly institutionalized approach creates an "information silo" where corporate HQ is unaware of ground-level dissatisfaction

Summary of Literature Gaps

  • No comparative study exists evaluating CG vs JH on communication metrics.
  • The vast majority of literature relies on secondary financial data, ignoring the beneficiary's voice.
  • Public relations theories have not been adequately tested in low-literacy tribal environments.
  • The link between how a project is communicated and how its impact is perceived remains empirically untested.
